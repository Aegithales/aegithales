Unstructured Complexity

  • There are many measures of complexity: Kolmogorov complexity, computational complexity, Krohn–Rhodes complexity, Linguistic sequence complexity, etc.

  • But I want a complexity measure that reflects our ability to explain / comprehend something with human cognition, using our capacity to represent / explain a system / phenomenon by combining well-understood building blocks.

  • Eg well-written software has an easy-to-understand structure, composed of modules with well-defined API and purpose.

  • But some natural systems might lack this modular structure, bearing a complexity that is impenetrable for the human mind, like

    • embryonic development;
    • metamorphosis of insects;
    • how instinctive behaviour is encoded in the genome and the CNS of animals;
    • evolution of some highly complex traits;

  • Note that this is not about size. A system may be huge, and still easy to understand. It is about structure or the lack of it.

  • My first attempt is what I call diagrammatic complexity: a ‘diagram’ is a graph of nodes that are interconnected by labeled edges.

    A ‘node’ may stand for a subgraph, which is then presented on another diagram.

    Decomposing the full diagram into smaller diagrams is only possible if the diagram has some regularity / structure.

    The complexity is the size the of the largest diagram after the decomposition.

  • Back to our examples: a well-written software is obviously decomposable into smaller ‘diagrams’, like classes, functions, etc. that are re-used.

    But the genomic features, controlling development or metamorphosis, may lack such a modular structure.

    Which means we might be completely unable to understand it.

  • I hope that some important systems are still within the bounds of structured complexity, amenable for human cognition:

    • our brain and our mind itself;
    • and the system of tissues / organs of the adult human body (as a static system, excluding development).
