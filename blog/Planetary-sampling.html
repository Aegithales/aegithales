Planetary sampling by space travel

Imagine an explorer, trying to find out the probability of life on an ‘earth-like planet’ (ELP). She has access to unlimited space travel, able to ‘teleport’ to any remote galaxies instantaneously.

Her method is sampling the planets randomly, and checking for signs of carbon-based life. After visiting a large number of planets, let’s say NtotalN_{total}, she can derive the transitional probability of abiogenesis, as TRP=Nlife/NtotalTRP = N_{life} / N_{total}.

This method is theoretically perfect, but has many flaws in practice. If the transitional probability from barren to life is very low, there is a chance that our researcher will not find any life form, even after visiting a million planets. So she will conclude that TRP=0TRP = 0, even if it is obviously not the case.

Also, assuming the space travel has a range limit, she will mostly explore the galaxies in vicinity of her home planet, which is a non-uniform sampling, resulting in a biased estimation.

But what is really crazy, is that this bias remains even if we remove the range constraint of teleportation. It is enough that the explorer has to choose her destination: - it will always be in the ‘vicinity’ or her home planet, when considering the vastness of an infinite universe.

If you doubt it, consider the analogous problem of selecting a random postitive integer. We are inclined to select a ‘small’ one, even though 99.999% of the positive integers have more than one billion digits. Actually, there is no way to even define a uniform probability distribution over the positive integers.

If the universe in infinite in space, as it is now the most probable cosmological model, then planetary sampling by space travel will never be an unbiased, uniform sampling.

Planetary sampling by reincarnation

Now stretching our thought experiment even further, let’s assume that instead of space travel, the researcher explores the universe by reincarnating on different planets in each of her lives.

Being born on a certain planet has no range limit, so this is a spatially uniform sampling of an infinite Universe.

One can object that reincarnation is a fictional concept, but we may counter that every human being is born at least once. Even if that is not reincarnation, it is a way of planetary sampling, selecting an earth-like planet randomly in an infinite universe.

Admittedly, the sample size (1) is very small, yet it can yield a crude estimation of the TRP(barren→life)TRP(barren \rightarrow life).

But now we are facing another kind of bias: anthropic selection. Obviously humans always reincarnates on planets with life, so our researcher will always find that her home planet has highly evolved life forms, and even intelligent life (herself).

Planetary sampling of strange life-forms

So sampling planets for abiogenesis (or even intelligent life form) is biased by anthropic selection.

How about sampling planets to check if a certain life-form has developed on that planet? A life-form that is not ‘needed’ as an intermediate species to eventually evolve humans?

Let’s take, for example, a curious fish species, the Stargazer fish. It is highly adapted to its special way to lure its prey near to its mouth and stun it with an electrical shock, while burying itself into the ocean floor:

We can say that the Stargazer fish, evolving on a certain planet, is an independent event from the appearance of intelligent life, so anthropic selection does not bias this observation.

One would expect that only the minimally required other life forms (bacteria, plants, etc) would be observed by our reincarnated researcher, as these exotic, strange life forms have very low TRPs, so an unbiased planetary sampling would not find them at all.

But strangely, our planet Earth is abound of these exotic creatures on evolutionary side-branches (not on the main branch, leading to humans). Why don’t we observe a lean, frugal evolutionary tree, with only a few ‘other’ species?

Again, there is a subtle sampling bias here. I have chosen the Stargazer fish, as an ‘exotic’ animal, - but I did not choose it randomly. I knew about it, I was fascinated by this fish, so in a way, I chose to test the existence of a species that I knew it was present on Earth.

Planetary sampling of the shape of the Tree of Life

Ok, forget about the Stargazer fish. Let’s sample planets with intelligent life, and check if the Tree of Life is a long stalk with very few branches, ending in the Homo Sapiens node, or a large tree spreading laterally, with many side branches.

It seems that a large, bushy tree would be less probable, as each point of branching adds a factor of the transitional probability of that branching, which is small. So less branching, higher probability…

Maybe, but do we sample properly the total probability of observing a tree of a certain shape, by the assumed probabilities of each branching events?

To illustrate the bias in this computation, let’s have a look at the long, barren tree with as few side branches as possible: we assume it has the highest probability. But we did not count in the probability of not branching at any point in time: which is a big product of factors like (1−TRPi)(1 - TRP_i) for each transitional events ii that did not happen. If there are lots of such events, the product is close to zero, lowering the total probability of the long, slim tree.
