“we make our world significant by the courage of our questions and the depth of our answers” ― Carl Sagan, Cosmos

One striking way to contrast the popular imagination of how evolution works with reality is by examining bio-inspired evolutionary algorithms. These blind search processes generate a wide variety of potential designs — often by combining features from earlier candidates — and then evaluate or test them extensively, maintaining a diverse pool of alternatives throughout.

This bears little resemblance to actual biological evolution, where species tend to be remarkably stable, often persisting for over 100 million years. There isn’t a fuzzy blob of slightly varied “candidates” being tested for survival. Instead, speciation appears to be a relatively sudden event when viewed against the vast timescale of the fossil record.

To search for optimal, nontrivial design we need to explore a huge number of alternative concurrently. The Darwinian theory assumes that this concurrent, parallel search happens here, on Earth, in the form of mutated individuals that fail to survive. But even if we can sometimes find an example for adaptive mutations, they are the exceptions, and so few that they cannot make up the bulk of the huge number of failure that a real search would produce.

On these pages, I would like to explore that idea that evolution happens concurrently on an infinite number of Earth-like planets, scattered around the infinite space of the Universe. In most cases, evolution (and even abiogenesis) fails on these planets, but given the actual infinity of space, there are some where extremely unlikely events conicide and accumulate, creating life and ultimately, intelligent beings.

Naturally, we can observe only one of these planets, where evolution succeeded, and we don’t see all the others where it failed. This is called anthropic selection. So the blind search by mutational events does occur concurrently on infinite number of planets, but we never get to see the complete ‘fuzzy blob’ of alternatives, most of which occur on extremely remote galaxies.
