My list of important unsolved problems in science

From my brief stay at an academic institution as a doctoral student, I recall that many great scientific problems are unsuited for funding, and consequently, of any serious attention. The reasons vary:

  • Some problems are too theoretical, with no practical application;

  • or too difficult, unlikely to succeed, and reporting failure will not get another round of funding;

  • or if some ‘solution’ exists already, maybe as an overly optimistic report to fake success on a failed project;

  • or otherwise the presence of an old, often sketchy theory that should have been discarded long ago, but now it is difficult to explain to sponsors why to research a question for which we already has an answer.

  • Also, researchers are expected to be humble and modest, no one likes a young guy who would want to tackle the ‘big’ problems in science.

So here is my list of top-ten-or-twenty-something scientific problems, on whic I would fund research if I were a billionaire:

Evolution and abiogenesis

  1. Evolution: while natural selection is obviously in play, some features are too complex to be developed gradually. Also, incomplete version of these features don’t carry any survival benefits, so selection cannot explain their appearance.

  2. Speciation: why species are so stable? If mutations and genetic drifting is the primary way of evolution, why don’t we see a spectrum of different animals, each diverging from its ancestors a little, exploring the fitness landscape?

  3. The problem of Extinction: it seems that some species survive despite obvious weaknesses. Eg thorny and toxic plants have a huge survival advantage over ‘edible’ plants, yet common grass is still prevalent. Where is natural selection?

    Somehow extinction is random, and it is triggered by some ‘internal’ mechanism: all species go extinct after some 100-200 million years.

  4. The problem of abiogenesis: how life arose on Earth almost instantly after oceans formed, as LUCA (the Last Universal Common Ancestor) already contained the fully developed transcriptional / translational machinary?

Theory of Mind

  1. Given a neural network, trained with backpropagation and/or reinforcement learning, what is a formalism that would describe the resulting internal representation? I want a formalism that is invariant to the specific random end-state of the learning, that is, the actual weight matric, but reveals the invariant structure that specifically represent a given input.

  2. This is related to the general question if the human mind is amenable to understanding with scientific concepts, that is if it has modular structure. Or it has unstructured complexity that cannot be dissected and comprehended by analytical thinking.
