My list of important unsolved problems in science

I always found that the Millenium Prize Problems underwhelming. I know, I know, these are problems in mathematics, not in science in general, - but they are more like unsolved remnants of some already explored theories than actual hot problems that we need to answer to understand our true nature or origin as human beings.

So here is my list of ten or twenty-something unsolved scientific problems that I find important and profound:

  1. Abiogenesis: how life arose on Earth almost instantly after oceans formed, as LUCA (the Last Universal Common Ancestor) already contained the fully developed transcriptional / translational machinery?

  2. Evolution: why species are so stable? If mutations and genetic drifting is the primary way of evolution, achieving swarm intelligence why don’t we see that ‘swarm’ of variants, each diverging from its ancestors a little, exploring the fitness landscape?

  3. True infinity of the cosmos (in space): is there a way to prove it? Does it have observable effects? Hint: it does…

  4. The side-branch complexity problem: while probabilities, leading to intelligent beings, are amplified to 1 by the ‘anthropic’ principle, why do we see probability amplification on the side-branches, not needed for humans?

  5. Morphogenesis: how can be the development of adult form so unerring, so accurate, unaffected by noise, not accumulating errors, even though it has no feedback on the shape and size and structure of organs and limbs?

  6. How instintive behaviours are encoded in the genome? For example, spiders don’t learn how to weave orb webs, they just know it…

  7. Perception is the process that incrementally build / maintain an internal representation of our surroundings, by processing sensory (mostly visual) input.

    What is the neural architecture that is capable to integrate, not only the visuals, but also prior knowledge of familiar objects, persons, and locations into this representation?

  8. I understand how classes of entities are recognized by networks of neurons, and how these classifiers are trained from data.

    But how the instances of these classes are learned, often from a single encounter?

    For example, the notion of a dog or a cat corresponds to a spot or a cluster of spots in ‘feature space’.

    But how the knowledge of a specific dog or cat is stored? It is unlikely that a classifier is trained for each instance of objects, animals, etc in our brain, yet we easily remember thousands of such instances.

  9. Intent: how intent is formed? How is it combined with perception to select and control the action?

    How innate goals / desires (like mating, survival, etc) restrict or give rise to intent and actions?

  10. A generic framework to describe neural architectures, allowing to create ‘blueprints’ of the brain, and prove or disprove statements (‘theorems’) about it.

    The framework should be agnostic of the underlying neuron models (spiking, McCulloch–Pitts / MCP, …), or the actual neural encoding…

  11. What is the architecture / model component (in this putative framework) that allows rehearsal of actions in the mind?

    How we imagine storylines, enjoy novels, daydream, or recall events, recreating perception states, without sensory input?

  12. How sleeping and dreaming can be modeled in this framework?

  13. How mental disorders, like depression, psychosis, etc can be modeled?

    How can we model neurodevelopmental disorders, like intellectual disability, motor disorders, etc?

  14. Besides brain / CNS substructures, the framework should allow the description / modeling of putative implants in the brain.

    Then we may ask the grand question if it is possible to ‘fix’ some mental disorders through implants,

  15. or even if some part of semantic / episodic memory, being transferred into the implants, which would act like a cortical area.

  16. Would it be possible for a brain implant to gradually take over large areas of the cortex, and ultimately, transfer consciousness into the chip, seemlessly, in a waking state?

  17. Would a chip like this allow the transfer of the patient’s consciousness into a computer system, maintaining this consciousness beyond the death of the patient’s body?
